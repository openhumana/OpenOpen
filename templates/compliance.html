What is TCPA?

The Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA) is a federal law enacted in 1991 that regulates telemarketing calls, auto-dialed calls, prerecorded calls, text messages, and unsolicited faxes. The TCPA was designed to protect consumers from unwanted and intrusive communications.

Key provisions of the TCPA include:

  • Restrictions on the use of automatic telephone dialing systems (ATDS)
  • Requirements for prior express consent before making certain types of calls
  • Regulations regarding prerecorded or artificial voice messages
  • Maintenance of company-specific Do Not Call (DNC) lists
  • Compliance with the National Do Not Call Registry
  • Time-of-day restrictions for telemarketing calls (8 AM to 9 PM local time)

Our Compliance Measures

While compliance is ultimately your responsibility, Open Humana provides tools and features designed to help you stay compliant:

  • DNC List Management: Built-in tools to upload, manage, and maintain your internal Do Not Call lists directly within the platform.
  • Auto-Scrubbing: Automatic scrubbing of your contact lists against your internal DNC lists before any campaign launches, preventing calls to numbers that have opted out.
  • Call Logging: Comprehensive logging of all calls, including timestamps, durations, and outcomes, providing an audit trail for compliance purposes.
  • Consent Tracking: Tools to document and track consent records for each contact, helping you maintain verifiable proof of authorization.

  • Caller ID Compliance: Proper caller ID transmission on all outbound calls.

User Responsibilities

When you use Open Humana's Digital Employee services, you act as the "Employer" and are responsible for all calls made on your behalf. Your key responsibilities include:

  • Obtain proper consent from all individuals before initiating automated calls or voicemail drops
  • Maintain accurate and up-to-date Do Not Call lists
  • Honor opt-out requests within the required timeframe
  • Comply with all applicable state-level telemarketing laws, which may be stricter than federal requirements
  • Keep records of consent for a minimum of 5 years
  • Ensure all calls are made within permitted hours (8 AM to 9 PM in the recipient's local time zone)
  • Provide accurate caller ID information on all outbound calls

Consent Requirements

Under the TCPA, prior express written consent is required before making telemarketing calls using an automatic dialing system or prerecorded voice. This consent must:

  • Be in writing (electronic signatures are acceptable)
  • Clearly authorize the specific type of communication (calls, texts, voicemails)
  • Include the telephone number to be contacted
  • Include the name of the party giving consent
  • Not be a condition of purchasing any goods or services

For informational (non-marketing) calls, prior express consent (which can be oral) is generally sufficient. However, we strongly recommend documenting all consent in writing for all automated communications to maintain a clear compliance record.

Do-Not-Call Lists

The National Do Not Call Registry is managed by the Federal Trade Commission (FTC). As a user of Open Humana's services, you must:

  • Check and scrub your calling lists against the National DNC Registry at least every 31 days
  • Maintain your own internal DNC list of individuals who have requested not to be called
  • Honor all opt-out requests within 30 days of receipt
  • Train all personnel involved in telemarketing on DNC compliance procedures
  • Keep records demonstrating your DNC compliance efforts

Penalties for Non-Compliance

Violations of the TCPA can result in significant financial penalties:

  • $500 per violation for each call made in violation of TCPA regulations
  • Up to $1,500 per violation for willful or knowing violations (treble damages)
  • Violations are assessed on a per-call basis, meaning a campaign of 1,000 non-compliant calls could result in penalties ranging from $500,000 to $1,500,000
  • Class action lawsuits are common in TCPA cases and can result in multi-million dollar settlements
  • State attorneys general may also bring enforcement actions with additional penalties

These penalties apply to the entity on whose behalf the calls are made — not to the technology provider.
